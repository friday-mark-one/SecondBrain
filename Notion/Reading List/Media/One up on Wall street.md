**One up on wall street**

**Peter Lynch**

**General advice**

- If you can follow only one bit of data, follow the earnings (assuming the company has earnings). What the stock price does today or tomorrow is only a distraction
- Homework before investing - earnings prospects, financial conditions, competitive position, plans for expansion
- Role number one: Stop listening to professionals
- The trick is to not to trust your gut feeling but rather to discipline yourself to ignore them. Stand by your stocks as long as the fundamental story of the company hasn't changed.

**Part I SUMMARY**

- Don't overestimate the skill and wisdom of professionals
- Take advantage of what you already know (domain where you are expert at)
- Look for opportunity that haven't yet been discovered and certified by Wall street - companies that are "off the radar scope"
- Invest in a house before you invest in a stock
- Invest in companies not in the stock market
- Ignore short term fluctuations
- Large profits / losses can be made in common stocks
- Predicting the short-term direction of the stock market is futile
- Long-term returns from stocks are relatively predictable and better than returns from bonds
- The average person is exposed to interesting local companies and products years before the professionals

**Categories of stocks**

- **Slow Grower**
    - Usually large and aging companies
    - Pays a generous and regular dividends
    - Make sure the dividends are paid as expected even during recessions.
- **Stalwarts**
    - Stalwarts are better than slow growers
    - Provide good returns only if held for a long period (say 20 years)
    - These stocks offer pretty good protection during recessions and hard times
    - Check for its long term growth possibility
- **Fast growers**
    - Aggressive, new enterprises that grow 20-25% a year
    - A fast growing company doesn't necessarily have to belong to a fast-growing industry
    - The trick is to figure out when they'll stop growing
    - Make sure the product that’s supposed to enrich the company is a major part of the company’s business and has room for further expansion.
- **Cyclicals**
    - Sales and profits rise and fall in regular fashion
    - Autos, airlines, tire, steel, chemical companies
    - Must keep watch on inventories, the supply-demand relationship
    - Watch for new entrants in the market which is usually a dangerous development
    - The worse the slump, the better the recovery.
    - Eg. the auto industry,
- **Turnarounds**
    - These are cyclicals that went so far down in a down cycle
    - But they make up lost ground very quickly
- **Asset plays**
    - A company that sits on something valuable that the wall street has overlooked.
    - Eg. Pile of cash, real estate, companies with oil/gas/mineral rights

**PART II summary**

**Choosing a stock**

- “Any idiot can run this company” is one characteristic of a perfect company
- **The 13 attributes of a perfect company**
    - It sounds dull or even better, ridiculous
        - Eg. Automatic data processing vs **Bob Evans Farms**
        - **Pep boys - Manny, Moe and Jack**
    - It does something dull
        - Crown, cork and seal makes cans and bottle caps
    - It does something disagreeable
        - Safety-Kleen goes around selling machines that cleans greasy auto parts or in oil machines
        - Clear Shield makes plastic forks and straws - a business that any idiot could run
    - It’s a spinoff
    - The institutions don’t own it and analysts don’t follow it
    - The rumours about - It’s involved with toxic waste or the mafia
    - There’s something depressing about it
    - It’s a no-growth industry
        - Eg. Plastic knives and forks
    - It’s niche
        - Exclusive to the market like owning rock pit
    - People have to keep buying the product like drugs, soft drinks, razor blades, cigarettes
    - It’s a user of technology
        - Invest in supermarkets that installs scanner rather than investing in a company that makes automatic scanners
    - The insiders are buyers
    - The company is buying back shares
        - Alternatives to buying back shares are
            - Raising the dividend
            - Developing new products
            - Starting new operations
            - Making acquisitions

**Stocks to avoid**

- Hottest stock that you hear everywhere
- Beware of the next something - like next IBM, next McDonald’s - they never meet the expectation
- Beware the whisper stock - stocks heard through whispers throughout the neighbourhood
- Beware the middle man - Eg. if IBM decides to manufacture it’s own hard disk instead of relying on SCI, then SCI is in the brink of disaster.
- Beware the stock with the exciting name

**Why will a stock be more valuable tomorrow than it is today?**

- It always comes down to earnings and assets, especially earnings.
- Assets include real estate, cars, furniture, clothes, rugs, boat, tools, jewelery and everything that could go in a giant garage sale, if you decided to liquidate yourself and go out of business.
- Of course, asset should subtract outstanding mortgages, liens, car loans, unpaid bills, poker debts, etc.
- This results in positive bottom line or book value or net economic worth.

A quick way to tell if a stock is overpriced is to compare the price line to the earnings line. Buy when the stock price is below the earnings line and sell when stock price rises dramatically.

**The famous P/E ratio**

This number gives an idea to see if any stock is overpriced, fairly priced or underpriced relative to a company’s money making potential. The p/e ratio can be thought of as the number of years it will take the company to earn back the amount of you initial investment. A P/E ratio takes the share price of a stock and divides it by the company's annual net income per share. A stock trading at $30 per share with an annual net income of $3 per share would have a P/E ratio of 10. Generally, stocks with a higher P/E ratio are more expensive while stocks with a lower P/E ratio are cheaper.

**Why would anybody buy a stock with high P/E?**

The fact that some stock have p/e of 40 and others have 3 tells that investors are willing to take substantial gambles on the improved future earnings of some companies. You’ll also find that p/e levels tend to be lowest for slow growers and highest for the fast growers.

Need to investigate if p/e ratio of various stock you own are low, high or average relative to the industry norms. If you hear “this company is selling at a discount to the industry” then its p/e is at a bargain level. Track the p/e level several years behind to get a sense of its normal levels. **An easy rule of thumb is to avoid stocks with excessively high p/e ratios, with few exceptions.**

**The P/E of the market**

The stock market as a whole has its own collective p/e ratio, which is a good indicator of whether the market at large is overvalued or undervalued.

**5 ways to see if a company earnings will increase in the future**

- Reduce costs
- Raise prices
- Expand into new markets
- Sell more of its products in the old markets
- Revitalize, close or dispose off a losing operation

**THE STORY**

If it’s a **slow grower**, you are in it for the dividend. (why else would you own a slow grower). So the important elements of the script are

- Company has increased earnings every year for the last ten years
- It offers an attractive yield
- Never reduced or suspended a dividend
- In fact, raised the dividends during good times and bad

If it’s a **cyclical**, the script revolves around business conditions, inventories and prices.

- There has been a 3-year business slump, but this year things have turned around
- Closed off losing operation
- Cut labor cost

If it’s an **asset play**, insiders are buying, the company has steady earnings and there’s no debt to speak of.

If it’s a **turnaround**, then has the company gone about improving its fortunes and is the plan working so far? Sells off losing operations and goes back to doing what it does best.

If it’s a **stalwart**, then the key issues are the p/e ratio, whether the stock already has had a dramatic run-up in price in recent months, and what is happening to accelerate the growth rate?

If it’s a fast grower, where and how can it grow fast?

- Earning increase every quarter
- Company plans rapid future expansion
- Debt is not excessive

**Conversation with a stock broker**

1. How would you classify this stock?
2. What’s the recent growth in earnings?
3. What is the p/e ratio relative to the historic levels?
4. What makes the company a good buy now?
5. Are the existing shares making a profit?
6. Where’s the expansion coming from?
7. What’s the debt situation?
8. How will they finance growth without selling shares or diluting?
9. Are insiders buying?
10. Chart of stock price vs earnings for the last five years
11. What about the dividends? Have they always been paid?
12. What percent of shares is owned by the institutions? (the lower the better)

**PART III SUMMARY**

**Some famous numbers**

- **Percent of sales**
    - What does a product mean to the company? What percent of sales does that product represent?
- **Price/Earnings ratio**
    - The p/e ratio of any company that is fairly priced will equal its growth rate. Ask your broker what’s the growth rate as compared to the p/e ratio? If p/e ratio is less than growth rate, it is an attractive prospect. To calculate growth rate, take the annual earnings and calculate percent increase in earnings from one year to the next.
    - A slightly complicated formula to compare the growth rate with earnings while also taking the dividends into account.
    - (Long term growth rate + dividend yield) / p/e ratio
    - Value 1 = poor; value 1.5 = okay; value 2 = better
- **Cash position**
    - Light ah purila
    - But if there is some share in cash beyond doubt, then it is an attractive prospect.
- **The debt factor**
    - Debt vs equity (Long term debts are taken into consideration; short-term debts are mostly ignorable)
    - A normal corporate balance sheet has 75% equity and 25% debt.
    - Pay special attention to debt when investing in turnarounds and troubled companies.
- **Dividends**
    - The presence of dividend can keep the stock price from falling
    - If buying a stock for its dividend make sure the company is going to be able to pay it during recessions and bad times.
- **Book value**
    - Book value of a stock refers to the company’s assets minus its outstanding liabilities
    - This value should not be taken into account as is. If the assets do not have better resale value, then it does not really have a positive book value.
- **Hidden assets**
    - These are sometimes not clearly visible in a balance sheet. The asset price listed for the company maybe from several years ago but the value may have increased for the current market price. (eg. gold, silver, oil)
- **Cash flow**
    - Cash flow is the amount of money the company takes in as a result of doing business.
    - Free cash flow is what’s left over after the normal capital spending is taken out. It’s the cash you’ve taken in that you don’t have to spend.
- **Inventories**
    - Always check if inventories are piling up. It’s a bad sign to see inventories piling up faster than the sales.
- **Growth rate**
    - Increase earnings by lowering costs and increasing prices.
- **Bottom line**
    - It’s the final number at the end of an income statement: profit after taxes. This number is used for comparing companies within the same industry.
    - The company with the highest profit margin is by definition the lowest-cost operator and has a better chance of surviving if business conditions deteriorate.

**Phases of a company’s growth**

Check every few months to assess the phase of a company and make decisions accordingly

- The start-up phase (risky)
- The rapid expansion phase (safe)
- The mature phase or the saturation phase (dangerous)
